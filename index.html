About

I'm a PhD candidate in the Department of Biomedical Engineering at Tufts University, where I develop near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) methods for non-invasively measuring brain and muscle hemodynamics.

My research centers on dual-slope frequency-domain NIRS, a technique that improves sensitivity to deep cerebral signals while suppressing contamination from superficial blood flow. In my first-author work, I've shown that dual-slope measurements can detect functional brain activation directly, and, most recently, that a three-layer tissue model — one that accounts for the cerebrospinal fluid layer beneath the skull — reproduces real functional NIRS data far better than the simpler models typically assumed. This layered approach enables more accurate measurements of brain activity without requiring the large datasets that tomographic reconstructions typically demand.

This work is carried out within the Fantini Lab, alongside Sergio Fantini, Angelo Sassaroli, and Giles Blaney, who collaborate with me on instrumentation and methods for functional brain imaging and tissue characterization.

Teaching

My teaching centers on bridging the gap between abstract physics-based concepts and the biological systems biomedical engineering students care about, often through direct analogies, hands-on demonstrations, and applied problem sets. As a teaching assistant across Electrical Circuits and Biomedical Applications, Introduction to Biophotonics, and Recombinant DNA Techniques, I've redesigned recitations and assignments around active, scenario-based learning — from breadboarding circuits to hands-on microscopy demos to critically evaluating AI-generated answers against course material.

I'm also committed to closing resource gaps in STEM education: through the Tufts Biomedical Engineering Research Scholars (TUBERS) program, I helped create a foundations curriculum that levels the playing field for incoming summer researchers, now adopted department-wide. I've mentored six undergraduates, three senior capstone teams, and three high-school researchers.

Threads of Innovation: How Fiber Arts Shaped the Digital World

Tufts Osher Lifelong Learning Institute (OLLI) — Fall 2026 · 6 sessions

When we think of programming and computational algorithms, we rarely think of yarn, knitting needles, and embroidery hoops — yet the foundations of our technological landscape started with these tools of traditional fiber arts. This hands-on course examines the "women's work" that shaped our world, from the Jacquard Loom and the Apollo space missions to the smart fibers of today, blending history, engineering, and hands-on crafting.
